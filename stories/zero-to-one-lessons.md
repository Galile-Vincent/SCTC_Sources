# Zero to One Lessons from Student Product Building

Moving from zero to one is less about having the perfect idea and more about reducing uncertainty quickly. Our team struggled most when we tried to solve all future problems before shipping the first version.

## The Three Rules That Changed Our Progress

## 1. Decide on a Hard Definition of Done

For each sprint, we wrote one sentence that defined success. If a task did not directly support that sentence, we moved it to backlog.

## 2. Build Around Real Constraints

Instead of waiting for ideal tools or complete datasets, we designed around what we actually had:

- limited time,
- mixed skill levels,
- and changing requirements.

This forced better product judgment.

## 3. Review Every Demo Through User Value

In internal demos, we asked:

- Is the user outcome clearer than before?
- Is the interaction faster than manual alternatives?
- Can a new person understand the flow in under one minute?

If the answer was no, we revised scope instead of adding more features.

## What We Recommend to New Teams

- Keep a single owner for final product decisions.
- Schedule feedback sessions before your implementation is complete.
- Treat your first release as a learning instrument, not a final product.

The teams that improve fastest are usually the teams that are comfortable shipping small and learning in public.
